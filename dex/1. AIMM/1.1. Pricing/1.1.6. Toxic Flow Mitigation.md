# Anti-Toxic Flow Protection

> Mechanisms to mitigate adverse selection, stat-arb extraction, and JIT attacks

---

## 1. Overview

AIMM is **arbitrage-friendly** for rebalancing but **anti-toxic** for extractive flows. The protocol employs multiple defense mechanisms against common AMM attack vectors while maintaining capital efficiency.

> For coverage ratio definition, see [Inventory Management §2](/docs/1.1.1-inventory-management#2-coverage-ratio).

**Design Philosophy:**
- Arbitrageurs provide valuable price discovery → encouraged via fair spreads
- Toxic flow extractors exploit information asymmetry → penalized via dynamic fees
- Liquidity providers deserve protection from adverse selection
- MEV searchers can be allies, not enemies → Cooperative MEV rebate program

---

## 2. Toxic Flow Vectors

### 2.1. Adverse Selection

**Attack**: Informed traders execute against stale prices before oracle updates.

**Example**:
1. CEX price moves ETH from $2000 → $2050
2. Trader front-runs oracle update
3. Buys ETH at $2000 from pool
4. Sells at $2050 on CEX
5. Pool LPs lose $50/ETH

**Impact**: LPs systematically lose to informed traders.

### 2.2. Statistical Arbitrage vs CEXs

**Attack**: High-frequency traders exploit price lags between pool and centralized exchanges.

**Characteristics**:
- Sub-second execution windows
- Exploits oracle staleness
- Unidirectional (always against LPs)
- Risk-free profit extraction

**Impact**: Continuous drain on LP returns, especially during volatile periods.

### 2.3. Just-In-Time (JIT) Liquidity

**Attack**: Sophisticated actors add liquidity just before large swaps and remove immediately after, capturing fees without exposure to toxic flow.

**Mechanism**:
1. Bot detects large pending swap in mempool
2. Adds concentrated liquidity front-running the swap
3. Captures disproportionate fee share
4. Removes liquidity in same block
5. Passive LPs bear inventory risk, JIT bot extracts fees

**Status**: ✅ **Mitigated via Flow Guard**, see [Flow Guards §Layer 2](/docs/3.4-flow-guards#layer-2-flow-guard-block-level-mev-protection)

The `flowCooldownSeconds` parameter (default: 15 seconds) enforces a mandatory delay between deposit→withdraw and stake→unstake operations, making atomic JIT attacks impossible.

## 3. AIMM Defense Mechanisms

### 3.1. Volatility-Based Fees

Dynamic fees that increase during high volatility periods when adverse selection risk is highest.

> **Formula Reference**: See [Parametrization §2.1.2](/docs/1.1.7-parametrization#212-vega--volatility-sensitivity) for vega formula and [Internal Oracle §8](/docs/1.2.2-internal-oracle#8-volatility-tracking) for EMA mechanics.

**How it protects**:
- Volatility spikes → fees increase automatically
- Toxic flow becomes more expensive during attack windows
- Arbitrageurs pay higher costs during uncertainty
- LPs compensated for increased adverse selection risk

### 3.2. Asymmetric Spread (Deviation-Based)

Prices lag true market value based on directional inventory imbalance. Higher fees charged on the imbalanced side.

> **Formula Reference**: See [Parametrization §2.1.1](/docs/1.1.7-parametrization#211-gamma--inventory-sensitivity) for skew formula and [Parametrization §2.1.3](/docs/1.1.7-parametrization#213-lambda--momentum-sensitivity) for deviation surcharge.

**Asymmetry**:
- Pool overcollateralized (selling pressure) → higher fees on buys
- Pool undercollateralized (buying pressure) → higher fees on sells
- Fee increases proportional to imbalance severity

**How it protects**:
- Toxic flow typically unidirectional (exploits price lag)
- Imbalance indicates likely mispricing
- Higher fees on imbalanced side = adverse selection protection
- Rebalancing arbitrage encouraged (bringing pool back to equilibrium)

### 3.3. Dual-Window TWAP Oracle

Internal oracle resists manipulation via dual time windows and same-block rejection.

> **Full Documentation**: See [Internal Oracle](/docs/1.2.2-internal-oracle) for TWAP mechanics, B64 encoding, and volatility EMA implementation.

**How it protects**:
- Flash loan price manipulation → rejected (same block)
- Multi-block manipulation → expensive (requires sustained capital)
- Dual windows → harder to exploit both simultaneously
- Volatility measured from TWAP deltas → smooths spikes

### 3.4. Market-Aware Bi-Factor Fee

Combines protections into single adaptive fee.

> **Full Documentation**: See [Spread & Fees](/docs/1.1.4-spread-fees) for complete spread model and [Parametrization §5](/docs/1.1.7-parametrization#5-fee-parameters) for parameter details.

**Adaptive behavior**:
- Normal conditions: ~$S_v$ (volatility band)
- Oracle disagreement: +$U$ (deviation surcharge)
- Coverage-worsening trades: pay $S_v + U$
- Coverage-improving trades: pay $S_v$ only (arb-friendly)

### 3.5. Coverage-Aware Withdrawal Haircuts

Withdrawals during undercollateralization incur withdrawal haircuts, discouraging bank runs and adverse selection exits.

> **Formula Reference**: See [Parametrization §2.1.4](/docs/1.1.7-parametrization#214-haircut-suppressor--withdrawal-penalty-modifier) for haircut formula and [Inventory Management §5](/docs/1.1.1-inventory-management#5-withdrawal-haircut) for detailed mechanics.

**How it protects**:
- LPs can't exit at par during adverse selection events
- Early exits subsidize remaining LPs
- Prevents cascading withdrawals
- Incentivizes staying during temporary imbalances

### 3.6. Cooperative Arbitrage

> 🚧 **FUTURE WORK — NOT YET IMPLEMENTED.** Cooperative Arbitrage is on the BTR DEX roadmap, not yet shipped on-chain. The mechanism below describes **proposed** behavior; no Solidity implementation exists in the current release. Feature target: post-mainnet, Phase TBD. See [Manifesto §5.4](/docs/manifesto#54-innovation-4-cooperative-arbitrage) and [Foundations §15.4](/docs/foundations#154-the-cooperative-arbitrage-model).

Rather than fighting arbitrageurs, AIMM **would collaborate** with them through a **whitelisted, reputation-based rebate program** that aligns incentives.

**The Problem with Traditional Arbitrage**:
- CEX-DEX arbitrageurs extract value from pools by exploiting oracle staleness
- LPs bear the cost of price corrections (Loss-Versus-Rebalancing)
- Adversarial relationship: arbitrageurs extract, LPs lose
- No mechanism to reward arbitrageurs who help the pool

**AIMM's Proposed Solution: Cooperative Arbitrage**:

**Not MEV, but Statistical Arbitrage**: This mechanism would target **cross-exchange (CEX-DEX) statistical arbitrage**, not MEV in the formal sense (which manipulates block ordering). Cooperators would be **arbitrageurs**-a broader category encompassing MEV searchers, HFT desks, and medium-frequency stat arb traders.

**How Arbitrageurs Would Become Cooperators**:

1. **Whitelist application**: Arbitrageurs would apply to the DAO for Cooperator status at [btr.supply/coop](https://btr.supply/coop)
2. **Equal starting rebate**: All approved cooperators would start with the same rebate tier (e.g., 20%)
3. **Reputation tracking**: Performance would be measured by `reputation = total_donations / total_rebates`
4. **Tiered rebates**: Higher reputation → higher rebate percentage (up to parametric max, e.g., 80%)
5. **Revocation threshold**: Low reputation (<0.9) would lead to loss of Cooperator status
6. **Competitive dynamics**: All cooperators would compete on equal terms; best performers would earn highest rebates

**Parameters** (proposed, pool-level governance):
- `maxCooperators`: Maximum whitelisted cooperators (default: 10)
- `maxRebateBps`: Maximum rebate percentage (default: 8000 = 80%)
- `minReputation`: Minimum reputation to maintain status (default: 0.9)
- `rebateCooldown`: Cooldown period for claiming rebates (default: 1 week)

**How it would work**:
```
Standard arbitrage:
  Arbitrageur profit = CEX_price - pool_price - fees
  Pool gets = fees only
  LP cost = LVR (loss-versus-rebalancing)

Cooperative arbitrage (proposed):
  Arbitrageur profit = CEX_price - pool_price - fees + rebate
  Pool gets = fees + donations (from high-reputation cooperators)
  LP cost = Reduced LVR (faster price convergence)

Rebate accrual (per swap):
  accrued_rebate += spread_captured × cooperator_rebate_bps / 10000

Claimable after cooldown (prevents instant extraction)
```

**Reputation-Based Rebate Tiers** (proposed):
```
Example progression:
  Cooperator A: 1000 USDC donated / 500 USDC rebates = 2.0 reputation
  Cooperator B: 800 USDC donated / 1000 USDC rebates = 0.8 reputation

  → A would qualify for higher rebate tier (e.g., 40%)
  → B below threshold (0.9), would face revocation

Why donate?
  Higher donations → higher reputation → higher rebates
  → more profitable arbitrage (lower effective cost)
  → sustainable competitive advantage
```

**The DAO's Natural Advantage** (emergent, proposed):
If the DAO were to operate its own arbitrage bot and donate **100% of proceeds** back to the pool:
- Reputation = total_donated / total_rebates → maximized
- Would qualify for highest rebate tier automatically
- Would effectively front-run adversarial arbitrageurs (lower profit threshold due to rebates)
- **Would close the LVR loop**: arbitrage profits would return to LPs who suffered the LVR

This would create a **competitive but fair game** where all cooperators would have equal opportunity, but those who contribute most to the pool (whether DAO or external) would earn the best terms.

**Why this would protect LPs**:

| Factor (roadmap) | Without Cooperation | With Cooperation (proposed) |
|--------|---------------------|------------------|
| Arbitrage timing | Extract after oracle lag | Race to rebalance first (incentivized by rebates) |
| Inventory impact | Extract then abandon | Actively managed (donations improve coverage) |
| Price accuracy | Exploits staleness | Minimizes staleness (competitive delagging) |
| LP PnL | Systematic LVR | Drastically reduced LVR (donations offset losses) |
| Competition | Adversarial extraction | Cooperative improvement (reputation game) |

**Whitelist Rationale** (proposed):
- **Quality control**: Would ensure cooperators don't expose LP deposits to excessive external CEX risk
- **Inventory management**: DAO and trusted actors would carry inventory risk (bounded to small fraction of pool TVL)
- **Active monitoring**: Would track cooperator behavior, donations, and reputation
- **Credit risk management**: Would prevent cooperators from using pool as leverage source

**Important**: This would be **not fully permissionless**-it would require DAO curation and monitoring. However, it would avoid:
- **Oracle centralization** (UAMM, DODO, Swaap): No single point of failure or manipulation
- **Manager trust** (am-AMM): No single entity controlling all fees and arb profits
- **Builder dependency** (McAMM): No reliance on off-chain infrastructure or PBS
- **Complexity** (McAMM on multi-asset pools): Simple per-swap rebate accrual, no auction mechanics

**Rebate Accrual & Claiming** (proposed Solidity sketch, not deployed):
```solidity
// Per-cooperator accounting (in base tokens)
mapping(address => uint256) public accruedRebates;
mapping(address => uint256) public totalDonations;
mapping(address => uint256) public totalRebates;
mapping(address => uint256) public lastClaim;
mapping(address => uint16) public cooperatorRebateBps;  // Individual rebate tier
mapping(address => bool) public isCooperator;

// Reputation tracking
function getReputation(address cooperator) public view returns (uint256) {
    if (totalRebates[cooperator] == 0) return 1e18;  // Perfect reputation initially
    return (totalDonations[cooperator] * 1e18) / totalRebates[cooperator];
}

// On every swap by a cooperator:
if (isCooperator[msg.sender]) {
    uint256 rebateAmount = spreadPaid * cooperatorRebateBps[msg.sender] / 10000;
    accruedRebates[msg.sender] += rebateAmount;
    totalRebates[msg.sender] += rebateAmount;
}

// Cooperators can donate to improve reputation:
function donate(uint256 amount) external {
    baseToken.transferFrom(msg.sender, address(this), amount);
    totalDonations[msg.sender] += amount;
    // DAO reviews reputation and may upgrade rebate tier
}

// Claim after cooldown:
function claimRebate() external {
    require(isCooperator[msg.sender], "Not a cooperator");
    require(block.timestamp >= lastClaim[msg.sender] + rebateCooldown, "Cooldown active");

    uint256 amount = accruedRebates[msg.sender];
    require(amount > 0, "Nothing to claim");

    accruedRebates[msg.sender] = 0;
    lastClaim[msg.sender] = block.timestamp;

    baseToken.transfer(msg.sender, amount);
}
```

**The virtuous cycle** (proposed):
1. Cooperators would compete to arbitrage first (rebates would lower their profit threshold)
2. Competition → faster price convergence → smaller LVR window
3. High-reputation cooperators would donate proceeds → LPs would recover LVR losses
4. Better LP returns → more liquidity → tighter spreads
5. Tighter spreads → more arbitrage opportunities → more cooperation
6. Low-reputation cooperators would be revoked → only aligned actors would remain

We call these (proposed) participants **"Cooperators"** or **"Cooperative Arbitrageurs"**-transforming adversarial extraction into collaborative market-making.

See: [Manifesto - Cooperative Arbitrage](/docs/manifesto#54-innovation-4-cooperative-arbitrage)

## 4. Comparison with Other AMMs

### 4.1. Uniswap V2/V3

**Uniswap**:
- Static fees (0.05%, 0.3%, 1%)
- No volatility adaptation
- No inventory awareness
- JIT vulnerable (V3 concentrated liquidity)

**AIMM Advantage**:
- Dynamic fees respond to risk
- Inventory skew protection
- Dual TWAP manipulation resistance

### 4.2. Balancer V2

**Balancer**:
- Static fees per pool
- Oracle price feeds (external dependency)
- No built-in JIT protection

**AIMM Advantage**:
- Market-aware bi-factor dynamic fees
- Internal + external oracle redundancy
- Volatility-aware pricing

### 4.3. Curve V2 (LLAMMA)

**Curve**:
- Dynamic fees via internal oracle
- Inventory-based repricing
- No explicit volatility component

**AIMM Advantage**:
- Explicit volatility measurement and fees
- Dual-window TWAP (more manipulation resistant)
- Configurable sensitivity parameters

### 4.4. State-of-the-Art LVR Mitigation Comparison

AIMM's Cooperative Arbitrage fits within the landscape of LVR mitigation research as a **rebate-style, whitelisted revenue redistribution mechanism** that avoids the strong oracle and block-builder assumptions of competing designs.

#### 4.4.1. McAMM (MEV-Capturing AMM)

**What it is**: McAMM auctions the **right to be the first trader** in each block. Arbitrageurs bid for this slot, and the winning bid goes to LPs, internalizing MEV that would otherwise be extracted.[^1][^2]

**How it fights LVR**: When external price moves, the first-trade slot captures most arbitrage value. By auctioning it, LPs recapture what they'd lose to informed flow.[^2][^4]

**Pros**:
- Conceptually clean
- Minimal AMM changes
- Strongly LVR-reducing with competitive auctions

**Cons**:
- **Block-builder dependent**: Requires PBS (proposer-builder separation) and builders who respect the auction, only realistic on Ethereum mainnet with MEV-boost[^1][^4]
- **Anti-democratizing**: Professional MEV shops integrated with builders dominate; retail arbitrage priced out
- **Portability issues**: Thin auctions on chains without mature MEV infrastructure
- **Multi-asset complexity**: "First trade of block" must translate to "first batch" across N tokens, complicating auction and MEV search

**Why AIMM chose differently**: Cross-chain deployment requires solutions that work without builder infrastructure. McAMM is Ethereum-specific.

#### 4.4.2. am-AMM (Auction-Managed AMM)

**What it is**: Harberger-style auction for a **pool manager lease**. Winner pays continuous rent to LPs and in return controls fee schedules and captures all internal arbitrage profits.[^7][^8][^9]

**How it fights LVR**: Manager continuously arbs the pool vs. external markets, internalizing MEV while paying competitive rent upfront. LPs recover LVR via rent payments and optimized fees.[^5][^7]

**Pros**:
- Economically elegant
- Unifies LVR reduction and fee optimization
- Chain-agnostic (only needs on-chain auction)

**Cons**:
- **Centralizing**: Single large manager controls all fees and trading revenue at any time[^7][^9]
- **Manager trust**: Malicious or absent manager creates governance risk
- **Oracle dependency in practice**: Competitive managers need off-chain CEX feeds and HFT infrastructure
- **Anti-democratizing**: Smaller players locked out until they can outbid incumbent

**Why AIMM chose differently**: Manager trust and centralization conflict with AIMM's permissionless design goals. We prefer distributed competition among multiple cooperators rather than winner-take-all auctions.

#### 4.4.3. UAMM (Oracle-Driven AMM)

**What it is**: AMM that derives price from **external oracles** rather than pool balances, adding slippage only to manage inventory toward a target.[^11][^12]

**How it fights LVR**: If oracles reflect true market price, UAMM quotes near-oracle prices, leaving essentially **no arbitrage gap**. Classical LVR from stale prices disappears.[^10][^11]

**Pros**:
- Strongest LVR solution theoretically (if oracle is perfect)
- Eliminates arbitrage opportunities at the source

**Cons**:
- **Oracle-centric and fragile**: Wrong, manipulated, or lagging oracle → **unbounded LP losses**[^10][^11]
- **Single point of failure**: AMM safety = oracle safety
- **Centralization**: Oracle choice, update frequency, and failover are governance decisions
- **Lag still exists**: Even fast oracles (Pyth, Chainlink) lag CEX prices by seconds; LVR reduced but not eliminated

**Related designs**: DODO, Swaap, and other oracle-first AMMs share these tradeoffs.

**Why AIMM chose differently**: Oracle-first designs trade decentralization for LVR reduction. AIMM's HYBRID: internal TWAP primary + Chainlink base-token depeg circuit-breaker (not a quote fallback) provides redundancy without single-point dependency.

#### 4.4.4. AIMM's Cooperative Arbitrage

**What it is**: Whitelisted arbitrageurs earn rebates proportional to their reputation (donations/rebates ratio), incentivizing them to donate arbitrage proceeds back to the pool.

**How it fights LVR**:
- Rebates lower cooperators' profit threshold → they arbitrage faster → smaller LVR window
- High-reputation cooperators would donate proceeds → LPs would recover LVR losses directly
- DAO could run its own bot with 100% donation rate → maximal reputation → would close LVR loop entirely

**Comparison**:

| Approach | Portability | Centralization | Oracle Dependency | LVR Reduction | Permissionless? |
|----------|-------------|----------------|-------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **McAMM** | Low (PBS only) | Medium (pro MEV shops) | None | High | Yes (auction open) |
| **am-AMM** | High | High (single manager) | Practical (manager needs CEX) | Very High | Yes (auction open) |
| **UAMM** | High | Very High (oracle control) | Total | Highest (if oracle perfect) | Yes (trading open) |
| **AIMM Coop Arb** | **High** | **Medium (DAO whitelist)** | **None (internal TWAP)** | **High (via donations)** | **No (whitelist)** |

**Key differentiators**:
1. **No builder dependency**: Works on any EVM chain, not just Ethereum with PBS
2. **Distributed competition**: Multiple cooperators (max 10) instead of single manager
3. **No oracle single-point-of-failure**: Internal TWAP with optional external fallback
4. **Simple per-swap mechanics**: No auctions, no manager leases, just rebate accrual
5. **Emergent DAO advantage**: Any cooperator can compete, but 100% donation rate naturally dominates

**Tradeoff**: Not fully permissionless (requires DAO curation), but avoids strong trust assumptions of oracle-first or manager-controlled designs.

**Why this works**: AIMM's dynamic fee model already prices LVR risk via volatility and inventory components. Cooperative Arbitrage adds a **revenue redistribution layer** that returns extracted value to LPs without requiring perfect oracles or centralized managers.

## 5. Configuration

### 5.1. Asset-Level Parameters

```solidity
struct Asset {
    uint16 gamma;              // Inventory sensitivity
    uint16 vega;               // Volatility sensitivity
    uint16 lambda;             // Deviation sensitivity
    uint16 haircutSuppressor;  // Withdrawal penalty dampener
    uint16 minFeeBps;          // Floor fee
    uint16 maxFeeBps;          // Ceiling fee
}
```

### 5.2. Recommended Ranges

| Asset Class | gamma | vega | lambda | minFee | maxFee |
|-------------|-------|------|--------|--------|--------|
| Stablecoins | 5000-10000 | 5000-8000 | 8000-12000 | 1-3 bps | 50-100 bps |
| Major pairs | 10000-15000 | 10000-15000 | 10000-15000 | 5-10 bps | 100-300 bps |
| Volatile alts | 15000-25000 | 15000-25000 | 15000-25000 | 10-30 bps | 300-1000 bps |

### 5.3. Tuning Guidelines

**Conservative** (maximize LP protection):
- Higher vega → stronger volatility penalties
- Higher lambda → stronger inventory penalties
- Higher minFee → baseline protection

**Aggressive** (maximize volume):
- Lower sensitivity parameters
- Lower minFee
- Rely on haircuts for extreme events

**Balanced** (default):
- Moderate parameters (10000 = 1.0× multiplier)
- Caps at maxFee for tail events
- Tested against historical data
