# 1.2.4.5. Slippage & Price Impact

> Understanding execution costs and how to protect against unexpected price deviations

---

## Overview

When executing a swap, traders face two distinct types of execution costs:

1. **Price Impact**: The deterministic cost of trade size against available liquidity
2. **Slippage**: The unexpected deviation between quoted and actual execution price

Understanding the difference is crucial for setting appropriate trade parameters and choosing execution strategies.

---

## Price Impact (Deterministic)

Price impact is the **predictable** price movement caused by your trade consuming liquidity. It's known before execution and depends on:

- **Trade size** relative to available liquidity
- **Liquidity depth** at current price levels
- **AMM curve shape** (invariant-based vs. spline-based)

### Calculation

For a constant-product AMM (x·y=k):

$$\text{priceImpact} \approx \frac{\text{tradeSize}}{\text{poolReserve} + \text{tradeSize}}$$

| Trade Size (% of Pool) | Approximate Impact |
|-----------------------|--------------------|
| 0.1% | ~0.1% |
| 1% | ~1% |
| 5% | ~4.8% |
| 10% | ~9.1% |

### AIMM's Approach

AIMM uses **spline-based liquidity profiles** rather than fixed invariant curves. This allows:

- **Shaped depth**: Pool operators can concentrate liquidity where trading occurs most
- **Dynamic adjustment**: Depth scales with coverage ratio and volatility
- **Retail-friendly**: Tighter impact for small trades, wider for large

The `dispersion` parameter controls price impact curvature:

$$\kappa = \kappa_0 + \sigma \cdot \nu$$

where:
- $\kappa$ = dispersion
- $\kappa_0$ = base dispersion
- $\sigma$ = volatility
- $\nu$ = vega sensitivity

[^0]: **Solidity implementation**: `scaledVol = (volatility * vega) / (1000 * MULT_BASE)` where `MULT_BASE = 10000`, then `dispersion = 1000 + scaledVol` clamped to `[minDispersion, maxDispersion]`. See `LibPricing._calculateDispersion()`.

Higher volatility → wider dispersion → greater price impact protection for LPs.

## Slippage (Stochastic)

Slippage is the **unexpected** difference between quoted price and execution price. Unlike price impact, slippage is unpredictable and can be positive or negative.

### Causes of Slippage

| Source | Description | Direction |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **Market movement** | Price changes between quote and execution | Either |
| **Front-running** | MEV searcher trades ahead of you | Negative |
| **Sandwich attacks** | MEV bot manipulates price before and after | Negative |
| **Network latency** | Delayed transaction confirmation | Either |
| **Oracle updates** | Price feed changes during execution | Either |

### Positive Slippage

Slippage can be **positive** (favorable) if the market moves in your favor between quote and execution. However, this is rare on public mempools because:

1. MEV searchers monitor pending transactions
2. Favorable price movements are captured by backrunners
3. Only unfavorable movements pass through to the original trader

This asymmetry is why MEV protection matters, without it, traders experience systematically negative slippage.

## Protection Mechanisms

### 1. `minAmountOut` Parameter

AIMM's `swap()` function enforces slippage protection via `minAmountOut`:

```solidity
function swap(
    address tokenIn,
    address tokenOut,
    uint256 amountIn,
    uint256 minAmountOut,  // ← Slippage protection
    address recipient
) external returns (uint256 amountOut);
```

If execution would result in less than `minAmountOut`, the transaction reverts:

```solidity
if (finalAmountOut < minAmountOut) {
    revert ThresholdViolation(finalAmountOut, minAmountOut);
}
```

**Setting `minAmountOut`**:
- **Tight tolerance (0.1-0.5%)**: Protects against MEV but may fail in volatile markets
- **Normal tolerance (0.5-1%)**: Balance of protection and success rate
- **Wide tolerance (1-3%)**: Higher success rate but more exposure to slippage

### 3. Timing Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Trade-off |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Low volatility periods** | Trade when markets are calm | May miss opportunities |
| **Order splitting** | Break large orders into smaller pieces | Higher gas costs |
| **Limit orders** | Set exact price (if supported) | May not execute |
| **Off-peak hours** | Reduced MEV competition | Lower liquidity |

## Chain-Specific Considerations

### Ethereum L1 (Public Mempool)

Ethereum's public mempool makes all pending transactions visible to anyone running a node. MEV searchers continuously monitor for profitable reordering opportunities.

**Transaction ordering**: Gas auction, highest priority fee wins position in block.

**Protection**: Use MEV-protected RPCs (listed above) to submit transactions directly to trusted block builders.

### Arbitrum / OP Stack Rollups (Sequencer)

Transactions go directly to a centralized sequencer, which has full visibility of pending transactions but is expected to order fairly.

**Original ordering**: FCFS (First-Come-First-Served)
**Current (Arbitrum)**: Timeboost auction for priority lane
**Current (Base/OP)**: FCFS with priority fee escalation

**Protection**: Sequencer is trusted not to extract MEV directly. Third-party MEV is limited by sequencer control over ordering.

### Solana (Gulf Stream)

No public mempool, transactions forwarded directly to slot leaders. The leader controls ordering.

**MEV infrastructure**: Jito provides bundle auctions at the validator level.

**Protection**: Helius RPC offers optional Jito integration for MEV protection.

### Sui (Narwhal DAG)

DAG-based mempool with object-based execution model. MEV concentrated around "shared objects" (e.g., AMM pools).

**Ordering**: Parallel execution of independent transactions reduces ordering-based MEV for simple transfers.

**Protection**: Contention-based MEV still exists for AMM trades accessing shared pool state.

## Practical Recommendations

### For Retail Users

1. **Always set `minAmountOut`** to at least 99% of quoted output
2. **Use MEV-protected RPCs** on Ethereum and BNB Chain
3. **Check gas prices**-high gas often correlates with MEV activity
4. **Split large trades** if you're swapping more than 1% of pool depth

### For Integrators

1. **Quote with fresh oracle prices** before displaying to users
2. **Calculate realistic slippage bounds** based on volatility
3. **Provide RPC configuration** for MEV protection
4. **Show price impact clearly** before execution

### For Large Traders

1. **Use intent-based systems** (CoW Protocol, UniswapX) for better execution
2. **Consider private mempools** or direct builder connections
3. **Time-weight execution** across multiple blocks
4. **Monitor markout** to measure actual execution quality
