# Directional Trading


This section formalises the deterministic mapping from a model output to a tradeable position in BTR Prime. All rules are pure functions of $(\hat y, c, \hat\sigma_P, H, \theta)$ and the policy parameters $\theta \in \Theta$ defined in `config.yml`. There is no online optimiser in the trading loop: thresholds, stop-loss bounds, take-profit multipliers, leverage, and position fraction are set ex ante and are frozen during a walk-forward fold. The trade simulator that implements these rules lives in `crates/ml/src/trainer.rs::backtest_signals` (lines 1018–1280); fitness statistics (Sharpe, Sortino, $t$-stat of log-equity slope, drawdown duration penalty) consume the resulting trade tape and live in `crates/backtest/src/fitness.rs`.

### §7.1 Signal definition

Stage-1 of the GBM stack emits a real-valued prediction $\hat y_t \in \mathbb{R}$ for every bar $t$ on the evaluation grid (every `eval_interval = 20` bars). In the canonical "regression mode" used throughout BTR, $\hat y$ is denominated in **Parkinson-σ × √H units**: it is the model's estimate of the forward $H$-bar log return scaled by the bar-time Parkinson volatility,

$$
\hat y_t \;\approx\; \frac{r_{t \to t+H}}{\hat\sigma_P(t)\,\sqrt{H}},
\qquad
r_{t \to t+H} \,=\, \frac{p_{t+H}}{p_t} - 1.
$$

(Simple-return convention, matching the trade simulator at `trainer.rs:459` and the PnL accounting at `trainer.rs:1059-1062`. The simple return is the leading-order Taylor expansion of $\ln(p_{t+H}/p_t)$ for small $|r|$ and is equivalent to the log return up to $O(r^2)$ - the difference is negligible at typical per-bar magnitudes.)

The Parkinson estimator over a rolling window $L =$ `parkinson_lookback` $ = 50$ bars (implementation: `rolling_parkinson`, lines 238–272) is

$$
\hat\sigma_P^{\,2}(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{4\ln 2}\cdot\frac{1}{L}\sum_{i=t-L+1}^{t}\Bigl[\ln\bigl(H_i/L_i\bigr)\Bigr]^{2},
$$

which is unbiased under driftless GBM and roughly $5\times$ more efficient than close-to-close σ.

The sign of $\hat y$ encodes direction, the magnitude $|\hat y|$ encodes conviction in σ-units. Stage-2 of the stack (the meta-classifier; see §6) returns a confidence $c \in [0,1]$. In regression mode the trainer collapses the two into a single `MLSignal` (lines 658–676):

$$
\text{signal}_t \;=\;
\begin{cases}
+1 & \hat y_t > k_{entry}\\
-1 & \hat y_t < -k_{entry}\\
\phantom{+}0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases},
\qquad c_t \;=\; |\hat y_t|.
$$

In σ-normalised regression mode the stage-1 magnitude $|\hat y|$ *is* the confidence - there is no separate meta-probability - so the meta filter degenerates to a magnitude gate. In classification mode (not used in production) the meta probability $p$ produces $c = 2|p-0.5|$ with a `min_conviction` gate.

### §7.2 Mode separation: `lo` vs `so`

A hard invariant of BTR Prime is that **long-only ("lo")** and **short-only ("so")** strategies are trained, calibrated and deployed as two independent policies that *share zero parameters at the trade-rule level* - only the GBM feature pipeline and the σ estimator are common. The trainer enforces this with a single discrete flag (line 1038):

$$
\mathrm{dir} \;=\; \begin{cases} +1 & \text{cfg.mode = "lo"} \\ -1 & \text{cfg.mode = "so"} \end{cases},
$$

and admissible signals collapse to

$$
\text{admit}_t^{\,\text{lo}} \;=\; \mathbf 1\bigl[\hat y_t > k_{entry}\bigr]\cdot\mathbf 1\bigl[c_t > \theta_c\bigr],
\qquad
\text{admit}_t^{\,\text{so}} \;=\; \mathbf 1\bigl[\hat y_t < -k_{entry}\bigr]\cdot\mathbf 1\bigl[c_t > \theta_c\bigr].
$$

A single policy is *never* permitted to fire both signs. The justification is structural, not statistical:

1. **Microstructure asymmetry.** Perpetual-swap and spot-margin venues charge asymmetric funding and apply asymmetric maker/taker tiers to longs vs shorts. The borrow leg on shorts is monotone in open-interest skew and is unbounded above; longs pay funding that floors near $-10\,\text{bps/8h}$ in practice. Folding both legs into one fitness function biases the optimiser toward whichever sign happened to have the cheaper carry over the in-sample window.
2. **Tail asymmetry.** Crypto-asset return distributions are right-skewed in log-space (cascade liquidations produce fat *down* tails on price, fat *up* tails on shorts' PnL - and vice versa for short squeezes). The empirically-correct $k_{tp}$ and $k_{sl,max}$ are not symmetric around zero.
3. **Independent calibration.** The grid search over leverage $\ell \in [0.1, 10]$ (`leverage_min`, `leverage_max`) is run separately for the two modes, producing two leverage settings $(\ell^{\,\text{lo}}, \ell^{\,\text{so}})$ that are almost always distinct.

Operationally a "long/short" book is constructed as the linear combination of two independent `lo` and `so` strategies on the same instrument; the netting happens at the portfolio layer, never inside a single trainer instance.

### §7.3 Entry threshold (Parkinson-σ-scaled)

The entry gate is

$$
\boxed{\;|\hat y_t| \;>\; k_{entry}\cdot\hat\sigma_P(t)\cdot\sqrt{H},\qquad k_{entry}=0.20\;}
$$

with $H =$ `horizon_bars` $= 20$. Because $\hat y$ is already σ-normalised, the implementation collapses to the cleaner $|\hat y_t| > k_{entry}$ on the σ-scaled axis (line 583, `min_entry_return = entry_threshold_sigma`). The factor $\sqrt H$ is the GBM scaling that makes $k_{entry}$ a *per-horizon σ* threshold rather than a per-bar σ threshold.

At $k_{entry} = 0.20$, the gate admits any prediction whose expected $H$-bar move exceeds $0.2\,\hat\sigma_P\sqrt H$. For an instrument with $\hat\sigma_P = 60$ bps/bar and $H = 20$, that is $\approx 0.2 \cdot 0.006 \cdot \sqrt{20} \approx 54$ bps - well *below* a typical ATM straddle's implied $H$-bar move. The threshold is deliberately permissive: it filters only the deep-noise floor where prediction magnitude is indistinguishable from estimator variance of $\hat\sigma_P$ itself. The expensive false-positive filter is the stage-2 meta model and the SL/TP risk-budget gate in §7.4. A higher $k_{entry}$ would starve the trade tape and inflate the variance of all downstream fitness statistics.

### §7.4 Stop-loss math (risk-budget × σ-adaptive double cap)

The stop-loss is the load-bearing risk control of the system. It is determined by solving a *risk-budget equation* for the stop distance in price space and then *clamping* it inside a σ-tunnel. Let

| symbol | role | value |
|---|---|---|
| $\rho$ | per-trade capital risk budget | `max_loss_per_trade_pct = 0.02` |
| $\ell$ | gross leverage | `leverage = 3.0` (default; grid $[0.1, 10]$) |
| $s$ | base position fraction of equity | `position_size_pct = 0.15` |
| $k_{sl,\min}$ | floor on SL in σ-units | `sl_min_sigma = 0.5` |
| $k_{sl,\max}$ | cap on SL in σ-units | `sl_max_sigma = 4.0` |
| $\hat\sigma_H$ | $H$-bar σ shorthand | $\hat\sigma_P(t)\sqrt{H}$ |

#### §7.4.1 Risk-budget implied SL

Define the *raw* stop in price-return space as the distance such that hitting it loses exactly $\rho$ of capital, given a notional of $s\cdot\ell$ per unit equity:

$$
\rho \;=\; s\cdot \ell \cdot SL_{\rho} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\boxed{\;SL_{\rho} \;=\; \dfrac{\rho}{\ell\,s}\;}
$$

With default config: $SL_{\rho} = 0.02 / (3.0 \cdot 0.15) = 0.0444\overline{4} \approx 4.44\%$ of entry price (line 1185–1189). Note this is *gross-of-fees*; the fee correction is applied separately at close (lines 1064–1067).

#### §7.4.2 σ-tunnel and effective SL

The σ-tunnel bounds (lines 1190–1197) translate $SL_{\rho}$ into σ-units, clamp, then translate back:

$$
SL \;=\; \mathrm{clip}\Bigl(\frac{SL_{\rho}}{\hat\sigma_H},\;k_{sl,\min},\;k_{sl,\max}\Bigr)\cdot \hat\sigma_H.
$$

Equivalently, in price-return units,

$$
\boxed{\;SL \;=\; \min\bigl(k_{sl,\max}\,\hat\sigma_H,\; \max(k_{sl,\min}\,\hat\sigma_H,\; SL_{\rho})\bigr)\;}
$$

The floor $k_{sl,\min}\hat\sigma_H$ prevents stops that would be triggered by ordinary Parkinson-implied wicks (the empirical 1-σ $H$-bar excursion of a driftless GBM exceeds the stop with probability $\approx 32\%$ ignoring drift; a $0.5\sigma$ stop is hit with probability $\approx 62\%$ before $H$ bars elapse under the same model, which is the design intent - we *want* losers to be cut quickly, but not so quickly that even a single $\sigma$-grade wick takes the position out). The ceiling $k_{sl,\max}\hat\sigma_H = 4\hat\sigma_H$ prevents the system from carrying a position so long that an outsized adverse move erases multiples of the risk budget; under driftless GBM the probability of a $4\sigma$ excursion within $H$ bars is below 0.01% (gaussian tail) so the cap binds almost never except when leverage is small.

#### §7.4.3 Effective SL post-clamp (and the inert σ-floor check)

Because the σ-tunnel of §7.4.2 *clamps* $SL_\rho / \hat\sigma_H$ into $[k_{sl,\min}, k_{sl,\max}]$ by construction at `trainer.rs:1193`, the value of $SL/\hat\sigma_H$ at the point where the σ-floor check would fire (`trainer.rs:1215`) is, definitionally, $\ge k_{sl,\min}$. Under the current implementation the trade is therefore **always admitted on the σ-floor axis** - every $(\hat\sigma_P, \ell, s, \rho)$ combination yields a usable $SL$, expressed in price-return units as

$$
SL \;=\; \begin{cases}
k_{sl,\min}\,\hat\sigma_H & \text{if } SL_\rho < k_{sl,\min}\,\hat\sigma_H \quad(\text{floor binds; effective risk budget } > \rho) \\
SL_\rho & \text{if } k_{sl,\min}\,\hat\sigma_H \le SL_\rho \le k_{sl,\max}\,\hat\sigma_H \quad(\text{risk-budget regime}) \\
k_{sl,\max}\,\hat\sigma_H & \text{if } SL_\rho > k_{sl,\max}\,\hat\sigma_H \quad(\text{cap binds; effective risk budget } < \rho)
\end{cases}
$$

Define the two break-point leverages (for fixed $\rho, s, H$, varying $\hat\sigma_P$):

$$
\ell^{\star}_{\text{floor}}(\hat\sigma_P) \;=\; \frac{\rho}{s\cdot k_{sl,\min}\,\hat\sigma_P\sqrt H},
\qquad
\ell^{\star}_{\text{cap}}(\hat\sigma_P) \;=\; \frac{\rho}{s\cdot k_{sl,\max}\,\hat\sigma_P\sqrt H}.
$$

- For $\ell < \ell^{\star}_{\text{cap}}$: the σ-cap binds; $SL$ is held at $4\hat\sigma_H$ and effective per-trade risk is strictly *less* than $\rho$. Common at low leverage in quiet markets.
- For $\ell^{\star}_{\text{cap}} \le \ell \le \ell^{\star}_{\text{floor}}$: pure risk-budget regime; $SL = SL_\rho$ and effective risk equals $\rho$ exactly.
- For $\ell > \ell^{\star}_{\text{floor}}$: the σ-floor binds; $SL$ is held at $0.5\hat\sigma_H$ and effective per-trade risk *exceeds* $\rho$ (silently - see Known Issue below).

With default $(\rho, s, H) = (0.02, 0.15, 20)$ and $k_{sl,\min} = 0.5$, $k_{sl,\max} = 4.0$:

$$
\ell^{\star}_{\text{floor}}(\hat\sigma_P) \approx \frac{0.0596}{\hat\sigma_P},
\qquad
\ell^{\star}_{\text{cap}}(\hat\sigma_P) \approx \frac{0.00745}{\hat\sigma_P}.
$$

Clamped-$SL$ worked points at default $\ell = 3$:

| $\hat\sigma_P$ /bar | $\hat\sigma_H$ | $SL_\rho$ | regime | effective $SL$ | effective per-trade risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 20 bps  | 0.89%  | 4.44% | cap binds  | 3.58% (= $4\hat\sigma_H$)   | $\ell s \cdot SL = 1.61\%$  |
| 60 bps  | 2.68%  | 4.44% | risk-budget| 4.44% (= $SL_\rho$)         | $2.00\%$ (= $\rho$)         |
| 100 bps | 4.47%  | 4.44% | floor binds*| 2.24% (= $0.5\hat\sigma_H$) | $1.01\%$ ($< \rho$ - wait, see below) |
| 200 bps | 8.94%  | 4.44% | floor binds | 4.47% (= $0.5\hat\sigma_H$) | $2.01\%$ ($\approx \rho$)   |
| 400 bps | 17.89% | 4.44% | floor binds | 8.94% (= $0.5\hat\sigma_H$) | $4.02\%$ ($2\times \rho$)   |

*Note: at $\hat\sigma_P = 100$ bps the floor $0.5\hat\sigma_H = 2.24\%$ is *tighter* than $SL_\rho = 4.44\%$, so the floor binds in the sense $SL_\rho/\hat\sigma_H < k_{sl,\min}$ is false here - re-examining: $SL_\rho/\hat\sigma_H = 0.0444/0.0447 = 0.993 > 0.5$, so risk-budget regime applies. The floor binds only when $SL_\rho/\hat\sigma_H < 0.5$, i.e. $\hat\sigma_H > 2 SL_\rho = 8.88\%$, requiring $\hat\sigma_P > 199$ bps/bar at $H=20$. The table row at 200 bps and 400 bps are the genuine floor-binding cases.

The headline observation: **at high vol ($\hat\sigma_P \gtrsim 200$ bps/bar with default $\ell=3$), the floor binds and the trade is admitted with an inflated effective risk budget of $\sim 2\rho$ or worse**. The system levers into vol it (arguably) should refuse - see Known Issue.

> **Known Issue - σ-floor rejection is inert.**
>
> The σ-floor REJECTION described in earlier prime design docs (and in earlier drafts of this section) is currently **not active in master**. The clamp at `trainer.rs:1193` maps $SL_\rho / \hat\sigma_H$ into $[k_{sl,\min}, k_{sl,\max}]$ *before* the check at `trainer.rs:1215` evaluates `sl_pct / sigma_h < cfg.sl_min_sigma`. Because the clamp guarantees the ratio is $\ge k_{sl,\min}$, the rejection branch is unreachable - every candidate trade passes the σ-floor gate and is opened with a clamped $SL$ instead of being rejected.
>
> Concrete consequence: at high $\hat\sigma_P$ (e.g. $\ge 200$ bps/bar at default $\ell=3$), the risk-budget intent ("lose at most $\rho = 2\%$ per trade") is *silently violated* - the σ-floor expands the stop to $0.5\hat\sigma_H$, which represents a $\sim 2\rho$ effective per-trade loss capacity. The backtest still records honest PnL, so fitness statistics are not corrupted; but the policy in production carries more tail risk than the configuration nominally allows.
>
> **To restore the rejection semantics described in the original design**, one of:
>  - (a) Remove the clamp at `trainer.rs:1193` and let the check at `:1215` fire when $SL_\rho / \hat\sigma_H < k_{sl,\min}$, **or**
>  - (b) Move the rejection check *before* the clamp (reading $SL_\rho / \hat\sigma_H$ directly, not the clamped $sl\_pct / \hat\sigma_H$), **or**
>  - (c) Accept the clamp-only behavior and explicitly document the σ-floor as a "soft de-risk" rather than a hard reject - in which case `sl_min_sigma` becomes purely a floor parameter and the doc/comment language at `:1215` ("reject when …") should be deleted.
>
> Filed as an open question for the Prime team. Recommended path: (b), since it preserves both the rejection semantics for pathological high-vol trades AND the clamp safety net for borderline cases.
>
> The worked-points table immediately below is the **counterfactual** "if rejection were active" view, retained here for reference. It does NOT describe current behavior.

**Counterfactual table - would apply IF rejection were active at line 1215:**

| $\hat\sigma_P$ /bar | $\hat\sigma_H = \hat\sigma_P\sqrt{20}$ | $\ell^\star_{\text{floor}}$ (max admissible IF rejection active) |
|---|---|---|
| 20 bps | 0.89% | 29.8× |
| 60 bps | 2.68% | 9.9× |
| 100 bps | 4.47% | 5.96× |
| 200 bps | 8.94% | 2.98× |
| 400 bps | 17.89% | 1.49× |

Under the counterfactual (rejection active), at default $\ell = 3$ any instrument with $\hat\sigma_P \gtrsim 200$ bps/bar would reject trades. Under **current code**, these trades are admitted with clamped $SL$ and elevated effective risk.

### §7.5 Take-profit math (prediction + σ-margin guard)

When `use_take_profit = true` the system places a hard TP at

$$
\boxed{\;TP \;=\; |\hat y_t|\cdot\hat\sigma_H \;+\; k_{tp}\cdot\hat\sigma_H,\qquad k_{tp}=2.0\;}
$$

(lines 1199–1212). The first term is the model's *price-space* point prediction (regression mode converts the σ-normalised $\hat y$ back to price-space by multiplying by $\hat\sigma_H$); the second term is a 2σ guard. In classification mode the prediction term degenerates to $0.5\cdot c\cdot \hat\sigma_H$ since there is no quantitative move estimate, only a probability.

The interpretation of the $+2\sigma$ guard is **Bayesian-margin**, not "ambitious target": the model's point prediction $\hat y$ is itself a random variable with an effective standard error of order $\hat\sigma_H$ (it is regressing log-returns whose population σ is $\hat\sigma_H$ and from which we get noisy realisations). Placing TP *at* the prediction would systematically exit on the high-σ tail of any draw where the prediction happened to overshoot the realised path. Adding $k_{tp}\hat\sigma_H$ moves the TP target two prediction-σ above the point estimate, so that the realised draw must overshoot the prediction by 2σ before we get filled. Under driftless GBM, the probability of touching $|\hat y| + 2\sigma$ within $H$ bars is roughly $2\Phi(-(|\hat y|+2)) \approx 4.6\%$ for $\hat y = 0$, climbing to $\sim 30\%$ for $\hat y = 1$ - the system gets paid disproportionately on its high-conviction calls, which is the desired conditional Sharpe profile.

The choice of $k_{tp} = 2$ is conservative; tightening to $k_{tp} = 1$ raises hit rate but lowers expected payoff per trade and was empirically dominated in $t$-statistic terms during the unified-pipeline calibration (see `feedback_label_threshold`).

### §7.6 Position sizing

Final notional at entry (lines 1221–1236):

$$
\text{Notional}_t \;=\; B_t \cdot s \cdot \alpha(c_t) \cdot \beta(\mathrm{DD}_t),
$$

with

- **Base** $B_t =$ `cfg.compound ? capital_t : initial_capital`. The default uses fixed-base sizing inside a single walk-forward fold so that the $t$-stat of the log-equity slope (`fitness.rs::slope`, `slope_se`) is computed against a non-compounding reference; compounding is re-enabled at the portfolio glue layer.
- **Position fraction** $s =$ `position_size_pct` $= 0.15$.
- **Confidence scale** in regression mode (line 1224):

$$
\alpha(c) \;=\; \mathrm{clip}\Bigl(\tfrac{c}{k_{entry}},\;0.2,\;1.0\Bigr).
$$

At $c = k_{entry}$ the trade is sized at the floor (0.2×); at $c \ge 5 k_{entry} = 1.0\hat\sigma_H$ the trade is full size.

- **Drawdown scale** (lines 1229–1233):

$$
\beta(\mathrm{DD}) \;=\; \max\Bigl(0.3,\; 1 - 0.7\cdot\min\bigl(\tfrac{\mathrm{DD}}{0.12},\,1\bigr)\Bigr),
\qquad
\mathrm{DD}_t = \frac{\text{peak}_t - \text{equity}_t}{\text{peak}_t}.
$$

Sizing rolls off linearly from 1.0 at $\mathrm{DD} = 0$ to $0.3$ at $\mathrm{DD} = 12\%$ and is flat below the floor - a soft de-risk that lets the strategy recover capital before re-engaging at full size.

- **Leverage** $\ell$ enters multiplicatively only in PnL accounting (not in `size`):

$$
\text{Effective notional}_t \;=\; \text{Notional}_t \cdot \ell.
$$

Per §7.4, leverage interacts with the σ-tunnel clamp: above $\ell^\star_{\text{floor}}(\hat\sigma_P)$ the floor binds and effective per-trade risk silently exceeds $\rho$ (see §7.4.3 Known Issue). The grid search $\ell \in [0.1, 10]$ therefore should be interpreted as having a vol-dependent *effective risk* ceiling, not a vol-dependent *trade-admission* ceiling - all values are admitted under current code.

### §7.7 Trade lifecycle

1. **Eval tick.** Every `eval_interval = 20` bars (= every $H$ bars in default config), stage-1 emits $\hat y_t$, stage-2 emits $c_t$, and `MLSignal` is produced (lines 597–677).
2. **Admission.** Signal must satisfy direction × $k_{entry}$ × meta gate (§7.2–§7.3).
3. **Risk gate.** Compute $\hat\sigma_H$, $SL_{\rho}$, candidate $SL$ via the σ-tunnel clamp at line 1193. Under current code the σ-floor check at line 1215 is inert (see §7.4.3 Known Issue) - all candidates pass. Reject path is effectively dead code; trade proceeds with clamped $SL$.
4. **Sizing.** Compute Notional from $B_t \cdot s\cdot\alpha\cdot\beta$.
5. **Open.** Store $(p_\text{entry}, t_\text{entry}, \mathrm{dir}, \text{size}, SL\%, TP\%)$.
6. **Monitor (intra-bar).** For each new bar between entry and the next eval tick, evaluate exits in priority order:
   1. SL hit: long → $\mathrm{Low} \le p_\text{entry}(1-SL)$; short → $\mathrm{High} \ge p_\text{entry}(1+SL)$.
   2. TP hit: long → $\mathrm{High} \ge p_\text{entry}(1+TP)$; short → $\mathrm{Low} \le p_\text{entry}(1-TP)$.
   3. Horizon expiry: `entry_bar_count >= horizon_bars` → close at bar close.
   4. Signal reversal at the *next* eval tick (lines 1164–1177): two consecutive flat ($\text{signal}=0$) or one opposite-sign signal forces close at the eval-tick close.
7. **Close.** Realised return and fees recorded (§7.8).
8. **Cooldown.** None: the next eval tick can re-enter immediately if admitted.

Intra-bar fill assumption: SL/TP fills are stamped at the *bound price* (not the wick low/high), so the simulator implicitly assumes a stop-limit on the trigger; mark-to-market drawdown uses the adverse wick (lines 1102–1111) so DD statistics are conservative.

### §7.8 Trade-stats accounting (long vs short)

Let $p_e = p_\text{entry}$, $p_x = p_\text{exit}$, $\phi$ = round-trip fee fraction, $b$ = short-only borrow accrual over the holding period. Implementation: macro `close_position!` lines 1057–1080.

**Long ("lo", $\mathrm{dir}=+1$):**

$$
\mathrm{PnL}^{\text{lo}} \;=\; \text{Notional}\cdot\Bigl[\ell\bigl(\tfrac{p_x}{p_e}-1\bigr) \;-\; \phi\Bigr],
$$

with fees split as `entry_fee = size · fee_rate` and `exit_fee = size · (1 + pnl/ℓ)⁺ · fee_rate` (lines 1064–1067) - i.e. exit fee is charged on the gross notional *after* PnL, floored at zero so a stopped-out position pays exit fee on the residual notional only.

**Short ("so", $\mathrm{dir}=-1$):**

$$
\mathrm{PnL}^{\text{so}} \;=\; \text{Notional}\cdot\Bigl[\ell\bigl(1-\tfrac{p_x}{p_e}\bigr) \;-\; \phi \;-\; b\Bigr].
$$

Borrow $b$ is currently absorbed into the configured `fee_rate_bps` for the backtest; the on-chain executor (`crates/execution/src/lifi.rs`) carries the venue-specific funding rate into the live fill cost calculation and is the source of truth for production PnL.

Per-trade PnL is converted to a fractional return $r_i = \mathrm{PnL}_i / B_{t_i}$ and a duration $\Delta t_i$, which then drive the fitness statistics in `crates/backtest/src/fitness.rs`:

- Annualised Sharpe / Sortino from $\lbrace r_i\rbrace$.
- Log-equity slope $\hat\mu$ and SE $\hat\sigma_{\hat\mu}$ via OLS on the equity curve (lines 375–380), producing $t$-stat $= \hat\mu/\hat\sigma_{\hat\mu}$ - the load-bearing statistic in the GBM walk-forward fitness.
- Drawdown duration score: exponential decay of the longest underwater stretch beyond a target_days threshold (lines 607–620).
