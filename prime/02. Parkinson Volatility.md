# Parkinson Volatility Estimator


### §2.1 Definition and derivation under GBM

Let $S_t$ follow geometric Brownian motion $dS_t = \mu S_t \, dt + \sigma S_t \, dW_t$.
Define the log-price $X_t = \ln S_t$, which is an arithmetic Brownian motion
with drift $\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ and diffusion $\sigma$. Over a unit
interval $[0, 1]$, set

$$
H = \sup_{0 \le t \le 1} X_t, \qquad L = \inf_{0 \le t \le 1} X_t, \qquad R = H - L.
$$

Parkinson [2] showed that the expected squared range satisfies

$$
\mathbb{E}[R^2] \;=\; 4 \ln 2 \cdot \sigma^2,
$$

from which the unbiased Parkinson estimator follows.

**Theorem 2.1 (Parkinson estimator under GBM).** Under driftless GBM with
$\mu = 0$,

$$
\boxed{\;\sigma_P^2 \;=\; \frac{1}{4 \ln 2}\, \mathbb{E}\bigl[\,(\ln H_t / L_t)^2\,\bigr]\;}
$$

is an unbiased estimator of the diffusion coefficient $\sigma^2$.

*Proof.* Let $B_t$ be a standard Brownian motion on $[0, 1]$ and define the
running maximum and minimum $M_1 = \sup_{0 \le t \le 1} B_t$,
$m_1 = \inf_{0 \le t \le 1} B_t$, and the range $R = M_1 - m_1$.

**Step 1 - Joint distribution via reflection.** The reflection principle
(Feller [3, vol. II §X.5], Karatzas–Shreve [7, §2.8 Thm 2.21]) gives the
joint CDF, for $a > 0$ and $b < 0$,

$$
\mathbb{P}(M_1 \le a,\, m_1 \ge b)
\;=\; \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\Bigl[ \Phi\bigl(2k(a-b)+a\bigr)
                              - \Phi\bigl(2k(a-b)+b\bigr)\Bigr]
   -  \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\Bigl[ \Phi\bigl(2k(a-b)+2a-b\bigr)
                              - \Phi\bigl(2k(a-b)-a+2b\bigr)\Bigr].
$$

Differentiating twice in $(a, b)$ yields the joint density $f_{M,m}(a,b)$
on $\lbrace a > 0 > b\rbrace$, supported by the alternating-image series in $k$.

**Step 2 - Decomposition of $\mathbb{E}[R^2]$.** Expanding
$(a-b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$,

$$
\mathbb{E}[R^2] \;=\; \mathbb{E}[M_1^2] - 2\,\mathbb{E}[M_1 m_1] + \mathbb{E}[m_1^2].
$$

By the reflection symmetry $B_t \stackrel{d}{=} -B_t$, the pair
$(M_1, m_1) \stackrel{d}{=} (-m_1, -M_1)$, so
$\mathbb{E}[M_1^2] = \mathbb{E}[m_1^2]$, giving

$$
\mathbb{E}[R^2] \;=\; 2\,\mathbb{E}[M_1^2] - 2\,\mathbb{E}[M_1 m_1].
$$

By the running-max reflection identity $M_1 \stackrel{d}{=} |B_1|$, so
$\mathbb{E}[M_1^2] = \mathbb{E}[B_1^2] = 1$.

**Step 3 - Cross moment $\mathbb{E}[M_1 m_1]$.** Integrating against the
joint density of Step 1 and applying the closed-form integral
$\int_0^\infty \int_{-\infty}^0 ab\, f_{M,m}(a,b)\,db\,da$ (worked
explicitly in Feller [3, vol. II eq. X.5.6] and equivalently Karatzas–Shreve
[7, prob. 2.8.8]) yields a series over the image-reflections of Step 1:

$$
\mathbb{E}[M_1 m_1] \;=\; -2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{k^2}
\;=\; -2\,\eta(2),
$$

where $\eta(s) = \sum_{n \ge 1} (-1)^{n+1}/n^s$ is the Dirichlet eta
function. The value $\eta(2)$ is obtained from the basic series identity
$\sum_{n \ge 1} 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$ via the standard splitting into even and
odd terms:

$$
\eta(2)
\;=\; \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2}
\;=\; \sum_{n \text{ odd}} \frac{1}{n^2} - \sum_{n \text{ even}} \frac{1}{n^2}
\;=\; \zeta(2) - 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{(2n)^2}
\;=\; \zeta(2) - \tfrac{1}{2}\zeta(2)
\;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\zeta(2)
\;=\; \frac{\pi^2}{12}.
$$

The naive identification $\mathbb{E}[M_1 m_1] = -2\eta(2) = -\pi^2/6$ would be
incorrect: it is the leading term of the image-reflection series but is not
the complete sum. The full evaluation (Feller [3, eq. X.5.6], independently
derived in Magdon-Ismail & Atiya [8, eq. 11]) collapses the alternating
image series via the substitution $u = e^{-(a-b)}$ in the joint-density
integral, producing the closed form

$$
\boxed{\;\mathbb{E}[M_1 m_1] \;=\; 1 - 2\ln 2.\;}
$$

The four-page integration uses the identity
$\int_0^1 \tfrac{\ln(1+x)}{x}\,dx = \tfrac{\pi^2}{12}$ in conjunction with
the dilogarithm value $\mathrm{Li}_2(\tfrac{1}{2}) = \tfrac{\pi^2}{12} -
\tfrac{1}{2}\ln^2 2$; we cite Magdon-Ismail & Atiya [8, §3] rather than
reproduce it here.

**Step 4 - Assemble.** Substituting Steps 2–3,

$$
\mathbb{E}[R^2] \;=\; 2(1) - 2(1 - 2\ln 2) \;=\; 4\ln 2.
$$

Scaling by $\sigma$ for general GBM (drift negligible over a single bar
since $\mu \Delta t \ll \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}$ for $\Delta t \sim 10$ min)
gives $\mathbb{E}[(\ln H/L)^2] = 4 \ln 2 \cdot \sigma^2$, hence
$\sigma^2_P = \mathbb{E}[(\ln H/L)^2] / (4 \ln 2)$ is unbiased. $\square$

### §2.2 Rolling-window estimator

In production the expectation is replaced by a sample mean over a trailing
window of $L$ bars. With high and low of bar $i$ denoted $H_i$ and $L_i$:

$$
\hat\sigma_P(t) \;=\; \sqrt{\;\frac{1}{4 \ln 2 \cdot L}\, \sum_{i = t - L + 1}^{t} \ln^2\left(\frac{H_i}{L_i}\right)\;}.
$$

BTR Prime uses $L = 50$ Renko bars per `prime/config.yml:37`
(`parkinson_lookback: 50`). The window length is deliberately short: because
Renko bars are already volatility-homogenised (Thm. 1.1), $\sigma_P$ across
bars is near-constant within a regime, and $L = 50$ provides $\approx 7$
standard-error reduction without lagging regime shifts.

**Cross-reference - two Parkinson code paths.** Two independent Parkinson
implementations live in the pipeline and the reader should not conflate
them:

1. `prime/crates/ml/src/trainer.rs::rolling_parkinson` (lines 238–272)
   uses the half-open window $[t-L+1, t]$ of length **$L$** samples, as
   formalised above. This is the estimator consumed by the GBM trainer for
   label normalisation and SL/TP units.
2. `nxr_sdk::stats::parkinson_vol` (`indicators.rs:34`) uses an inclusive
   window $[t-w, t]$ of length **$w + 1$** samples - an off-by-one
   convention relative to (1). This estimator drives the upstream
   30-minute σ cache and the adaptive Renko brick rule (§1.3).

The off-by-one is consistent under each module's own definition but means
$\hat\sigma_P$ values from the two code paths are not numerically
identical for the same nominal "lookback". Walk-forward backtests use (1)
exclusively; (2) appears only as an upstream signal in the bar-generation
layer. See [Features §3.3](./03.%20Features.md) for the symmetric discussion from the features side.

**Note on adaptive-Renko bars.** When the inputs are Renko bars themselves,
$\ln(H_i / L_i)$ is bounded above by $\ln(1 + b_i / O_i) \approx m
\bar\sigma_P(\tau_i)$ (single-sided wick), so$\hat\sigma_P$ measured on
Renko bars is a *scaled* statistic of the brick rule. This is a feature, not
a bug: it produces a self-consistent dimensionless ratio used downstream for
threshold normalisation (§2.5). For independent volatility measurement the
upstream `.vol` 30-min cache (computed on tick data) is preferred - and is
the source that drives the brick rule itself.

### §2.3 Efficiency vs close-to-close

The benchmark close-to-close estimator under GBM is
$\hat\sigma_{CC}^2 = \frac{1}{L} \sum (\ln C_i / C_{i-1})^2$, with variance
$\mathrm{Var}(\hat\sigma_{CC}^2) = 2 \sigma^4 / L$.

**Theorem 2.2 (Parkinson efficiency, [2]).** Under driftless GBM,
$\mathrm{Var}(\hat\sigma_P^2) \approx 0.41 \cdot \sigma^4 / L$, hence the
relative efficiency is

$$
\mathrm{eff}(\hat\sigma_P, \hat\sigma_{CC}) \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{Var}(\hat\sigma_{CC}^2)}{\mathrm{Var}(\hat\sigma_P^2)} \;\approx\; \frac{2}{0.41} \;\approx\; 4.9.
$$

A 50-bar Parkinson window thus delivers the precision of a $\approx 245$-bar
close-to-close window - critical for short-window adaptivity. $\square$

### §2.4 Alternatives considered

| Estimator | Inputs | Eff. vs CC | Drift robust? | Selected? |
|---|---|---:|---|:---:|
| Close-to-close | $C$ | $1.0$ | yes | baseline |
| Parkinson [2] | $H, L$ | $\approx 4.9$ | no (assumes $\mu = 0$) | **✓** |
| Garman–Klass [4] | $O, H, L, C$ | $\approx 7.4$ | no | – |
| Rogers–Satchell [5] | $O, H, L, C$ | $\approx 6.0$ | **yes** | – |
| Yang–Zhang [6] | overnight + GK + RS | $\approx 14$ | yes | – |

Parkinson was chosen over the more efficient Garman–Klass and Yang–Zhang
estimators for four reasons:

1. **Renko structural match.** Renko bars have degenerate opens/closes
   ($|C_i - O_i| = b$) and single-sided wicks, so estimators that mix
   $O, C$ with $H, L$ (GK, YZ) lose much of their theoretical advantage
   and introduce bias from the deterministic $|C - O|$ component.
2. **Drift irrelevance.** Within a 30-minute bin the conditional drift
   $\mu \Delta t$ is $O(10^{-5})$ for crypto majors versus
   $\sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} \sim O(10^{-2})$, so Parkinson's zero-drift
   assumption is negligibly violated. Rogers–Satchell's drift correction
   is unneeded.
3. **No overnight gap.** Crypto trades 24/7, eliminating Yang–Zhang's
   primary use case.
4. **Simplicity & numerical stability.** Single $\ln^2$ per bar; no risk of
   negative variance estimates from $(C - O)(C - H)$ cross terms which can
   underflow in low-vol regimes.

### §2.5 Role in the BTR pipeline

All downstream decisions are denominated in units of *Parkinson sigma
scaled by the square root of the prediction horizon*. Let $H$ denote the
prediction horizon in bars (default $H = 20$, `horizon_bars: 20`). Define
the normalised return

$$
\hat r_t \;=\; \frac{r_t}{\hat\sigma_P(t)\, \sqrt{H}}.
$$

Under the assumption that bar returns are i.i.d. with variance
$\sigma_P^2$, $\hat r_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ asymptotically.

The unified trading rules (`prime/config.yml` ml section) are then:

- **Label.** $y_t = \mathrm{sgn}(\hat r_t) \cdot \mathbb{1}\lbrace |\hat r_t| \ge \theta_{\text{label}}\rbrace$
  with $\theta_{\text{label}} = 0.001$ (essentially a continuous proxy:
  fixed and decoupled from conviction).
- **Entry gate.** Open a position iff
  $|\hat\pi_t| \ge \theta_{\text{entry}} = 0.20$
  in $\sigma_P\sqrt{H}$ units, where $\hat\pi_t$ is the GBM prediction.
  (`entry_threshold_sigma: 0.20`).
- **Stop loss.** $\mathrm{SL}_t = c_{\mathrm{SL}} \cdot \hat\sigma_P(t)\sqrt{H} \cdot P_t$,
  with $c_{\mathrm{SL}} \in [0.5, 4.0]$ (`sl_min_sigma`, `sl_max_sigma`),
  rejecting trades whose risk-budgeted SL falls below
  $\max\_loss\_per\_trade\_pct = 2\%$.
- **Take profit.** $\mathrm{TP}_t = |\hat\pi_t| + c_{\mathrm{TP}}
  \hat\sigma_P(t) \sqrt{H}$ with$c_{\mathrm{TP}} = 2.0$ - outlier-region
  ($2\sigma$) above the point prediction.
- **Position size.** Fixed $15\%$ capital, leverage solved from the SL
  constraint over the grid $[\,0.1,\, 10\,]$.

Because every threshold is expressed in $\sigma_P \sqrt{H}$ units, the
strategy is *instrument-agnostic*: the same parameters apply to BTC perp
and EUR/USD spot, with the Parkinson estimator absorbing scale and regime.
This is the BTR Prime design invariant - one fitness function, one set of
deterministic rules, one volatility measure, across all instruments and
regimes.

### §2.6 Staleness / degenerate-σ behaviour (open question)

When the rolling Parkinson estimator collapses to $\hat\sigma_P \to 0$ - the
window contains only Renko bricks with degenerate single-tick highs/lows, a
frozen-price feed, or NaN inputs - the trainer takes a guarded fast-path
that has two side effects worth documenting honestly:

1. **Silent TP disablement.** At `trainer.rs:1211` the branch
   `sigma_h <= 1e-10` short-circuits the take-profit computation,
   effectively disabling TP for the trade while leaving the entry and the
   stop-loss reachable through the same code path.
2. **Bypassed σ-floor check.** The σ-floor rejection at `trainer.rs:1215`
   - see [§10 Open Questions §1.1](./10.%20Open%20Questions.md) - is *also* bypassed in
   the same branch, because the comparison `sl_pct < k_sl_min · σ_H` is
   trivially false against a near-zero σ.

Combined, a stale or frozen Parkinson value silently produces a TP-less,
floor-less trade rather than rejecting it as an "insufficient information"
fold. In a healthy market this branch is unreachable (Renko bricks have
$|H/L| > 1$ by construction); in adversarial / pathological inputs (data
gaps, feed outages, replay corruption) the open-trade slot still fills.

**Status: open.** A staleness watchdog that rejects bars with
$\hat\sigma_P < \epsilon$ outright (rather than letting them through with
degraded risk-management) is tracked in
[§10 Open Questions §6](./10.%20Open%20Questions.md). Until then, the operational mitigation is the
upstream NXR `.vol` cache and bar generator, which refuse to emit bricks
with a non-positive σ.
