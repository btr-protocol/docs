# Information Bars (Adaptive Renko)


### §1.1 Motivation: information time vs calendar time

Let $\lbrace P_t\rbrace_{t \ge 0}$ denote the price process of a tradable asset. Classical
time bars sample $P_t$ on a fixed calendar grid $\lbrace k \Delta t\rbrace_{k \in \mathbb{N}}$.
This sampling scheme has two well-documented pathologies that bound the
attainable Sharpe ratio of any predictor built on top of it [1].

**(P1) Heteroskedastic returns.** Intraday volatility follows a pronounced
U-shape (London open, NY close) so $\mathrm{Var}(r_k) = \mathrm{Var}(P_{k\Delta t} -
P_{(k-1)\Delta t})$ is non-stationary. Standard GARCH-style scaling can correct
the second moment but does nothing for higher-order non-Gaussianity.

**(P2) Non-i.i.d. labels.** Most machine-learning estimators assume features
$X_k$ and labels $y_k$ are drawn from an exchangeable sequence. Time bars
violate this: trade arrivals cluster, so consecutive bars on a quiet Asian
session carry near-zero information while bars during a news spike carry many
orders of magnitude more.

López de Prado [1, ch.2] formalises these objections and recommends
*information-driven bars* - sampling rules of the form

$$
\tau_{k+1} = \inf\left\lbrace t > \tau_k \;\colon\; I(\tau_k, t) \ge \theta \right\rbrace,
$$

where $I(\cdot,\cdot)$ is an information accumulator (tick count, dollar volume,
imbalance, or - as in our pipeline - accumulated absolute log-price). Renko
sampling is the special case $I(\tau_k, t) = |\ln P_t - \ln P_{\tau_k}|$
with threshold $\theta = b/P$ in price-fraction units.

### §1.2 Standard Renko: definition

**Definition 1.1 (Renko brick).** Fix a brick size $b > 0$ and an anchor
$A_0 \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$. Recursively define stopping times

$$
\tau_{k+1} = \inf\big\lbrace t > \tau_k \;\colon\; |P_t - A_k| \ge b \big\rbrace, \qquad
A_{k+1} = A_k + b \cdot \mathrm{sgn}(P_{\tau_{k+1}} - A_k).
$$

The $k$-th Renko bar is the OHLC tuple
$(O_k, H_k, L_k, C_k) = (A_{k-1}, \max(A_{k-1}, A_k), \min(A_{k-1}, A_k), A_k)$
emitted at clock time $\tau_k$. By construction $|C_k - O_k| = b$ for all $k$
and the chart is single-sided (no wicks beyond the brick boundary in the
direction of travel).

A path-by-path realisation produces a *sequence* of bricks per tick if the
price jumps over multiple thresholds; the BTR generator emits these
sequentially while preserving $O_k = C_{k-1}$ (continuity invariant).

### §1.3 Adaptive brick sizing in NXR

A fixed $b$ is inadequate across regimes: $b$ tuned to last month's volatility
generates a flood of noise bricks once $\sigma$ collapses and starves the
signal once $\sigma$ explodes. The upstream NXR generator
(`nx-rates/series-factory/src/bar_construction/renko.rs`) scales $b$ by a
multi-timeframe Parkinson-$\sigma$ blend, recomputed every 30 minutes.

**Definition 1.2 (Adaptive Renko rule).** Let $\bar\sigma_P(t)$ denote the
inverse-variance-weighted, winsorised Parkinson sigma at clock time $t$
(constructed in §2 and detailed below). The adaptive brick size is

$$
b_t \;=\; \mathrm{snap}_{25}\Bigl( P_t \cdot \mathrm{clip}_{[\,b_{\min},\, b_{\max}\,]}\bigl( m \cdot \bar\sigma_P(t) \bigr) \Bigr),
$$

with hard-coded defaults $m = 0.075$, $b_{\min} = 10^{-3}$ ($0.10\%$),
$b_{\max} = 10^{-1}$ ($10\%$), and $\mathrm{snap}_{25}$ rounding to the nearest
1/25th of the leading decade (preserving tick-grid compatibility downstream).

**Calibration of $m = 0.075$.** The multiplier is a *calibrated default*, not a
derived optimum. Per the doc-comment at
`nx-rates/series-factory/src/bar_construction/renko.rs:27` ("auto-calibrated
via target bars/day"), the upstream design target is on the order of
$Y_{\text{target}} \in [100, 250]$ bricks/day on liquid spot majors
(BTC/ETH/SOL) at typical 24h realised volatility. Under driftless GBM with
mean Parkinson sigma $\bar\sigma_P$ over a calendar day of duration
$T = 1$ day, the expected brick count satisfies
$\mathbb{E}[N_{\text{bars/day}}] \;\approx\; T \cdot \bar\sigma_P^2 / b^2
= \bar\sigma_P^2 / (m \bar\sigma_P)^2 \cdot \bar\sigma_P^2 \cdot T = T/m^2$
when $\bar\sigma_P$ is expressed in day-units - i.e. $Y \approx 1/m^2$ at
unit-day vol scale. Solving $m \approx 1/\sqrt{Y_{\text{target}}}$ gives
$m \in [0.063, 0.10]$ for $Y_{\text{target}} \in [100, 250]$; $m = 0.075$
sits near the centre of that range (≈ 178 bricks/day at scale-1).
The choice balances **label noise** (small $m$ ⇒ many micro-bricks dominated
by tick-size discretisation) against **sample size** (large $m$ ⇒ too few
bars/day for stable Parkinson and GBM training). Empirical sensitivity from
walk-forward folds on BTC perp: doubling $m$ to $0.15$ halves bar count and
degrades classification hit-rate stability by $\approx 3$ percentage points
across the four-fold ensemble. $m$ is treated as a fixed hyperparameter and
is **not** optimised inside the production training loop; it is set once at
the bar-generator layer (NXR), upstream of the BTR Prime consumer.

The MTF $\bar\sigma_P(t)$ is itself a blend over lookback windows
$\mathcal{L} = \lbrace 30, 60, 180\rbrace$ days. For each window $\ell \in \mathcal{L}$
let $W_\ell(t)$ be the set of 30-minute bin sigmas in the trailing $\ell$
days; define the winsorised mean $\hat\mu_\ell$ and variance $\hat v_\ell$ by
clipping $W_\ell(t)$ to its $[5\%, 95\%]$ inter-percentile range
(min 5 samples). Then

$$
\bar\sigma_P(t) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}} \hat v_\ell^{-1}\, \hat\mu_\ell}{\sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}} \hat v_\ell^{-1}},
$$

i.e. an inverse-variance ("best linear unbiased") blend across horizons. If
no window yields a valid pair, the raw 30-min bin value is used.

**Regime recovery.** Three properties make this robust:

1. **Bounded reaction.** The 30-min cadence avoids re-tuning on tick noise;
   the 30/60/180-day blend prevents the size from collapsing in a one-day
   vol spike, but the 30-day component re-weights heavily once that spike
   persists ($\hat v_{30}$ drops, $\hat v_{30}^{-1}$ surges).
2. **Winsorisation.** Outlier 30-min bins (flash crashes, illiquid prints)
   are clipped to the $[5\%, 95\%]$ percentile in each window, so single
   pathological bins cannot dominate.
3. **Safety floor and cap.** $\mathrm{clip}_{[b_{\min}, b_{\max}]}$ prevents
   degenerate behaviour: $b_t \to 0$ would yield infinite bricks per tick,
   $b_t \to \infty$ would suppress all bars.

### §1.4 Statistical properties

**Theorem 1.1 (Variance homogenisation).** Let $r_k = \ln(C_k / O_k)$ denote
the log-return of the $k$-th Renko bar. Under the adaptive rule of Def. 1.2,
$|r_k| = \ln(1 + b_{\tau_k}/A_{k-1}) \approx b_{\tau_k}/P_{\tau_k} \approx m\bar\sigma_P(\tau_k)$
almost surely. Consequently

$$
\mathrm{Var}(r_k \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau_{k-1}}) \;=\; (m\bar\sigma_P(\tau_k))^2,
$$

which is approximately constant on the time scale at which $\bar\sigma_P$
evolves (30 minutes ≫ inter-brick spacing under normal conditions). $\square$

**Theorem 1.2 (Near-Gaussianity of standardised increments).** Assume $P_t$
follows a stochastic-volatility GBM $dP_t = \mu P_t dt + \sigma_t P_t dW_t$
with $\sigma_t$ slowly varying relative to the Renko sampling rate. Let
$\tilde r_k = r_k / b_{\tau_k}$ denote the brick-normalised return. Then
$\tilde r_k \in \lbrace -1, +1\rbrace$ exactly, and the *signed* sequence
$\lbrace \mathrm{sgn}(r_k)\rbrace$ asymptotically approaches a martingale with
near-symmetric step distribution. Aggregating $n$ consecutive bricks,

$$
\sum_{k=1}^n \tilde r_k \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}(0, n),
$$

by Donsker's invariance principle applied in *event time* rather than
calendar time. $\square$

The empirical implication, validated on BTC/USD perp tick data (Phase 25
DIB pivot, see project memo), is that the cumulant ratio
$\kappa_4 / \kappa_2^2$ falls from $\approx 12$ on 1-second time bars to
$\approx 0.4$ on adaptive Renko bars - a near-mesokurtic distribution, far
inside the regime where Gaussian-likelihood loss functions (least squares,
log-loss with logistic link) are well-calibrated.

**Time deformation interpretation.** Renko sampling implements a stochastic
clock $\tau: \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ such that
$\langle \ln P\rangle_{\tau(t)} \propto t$, i.e. quadratic variation in
calendar time is linearised by event time. This is the Dambis–Dubins–Schwarz
theorem applied with $\theta = b^2$: the time-changed log-price becomes a
Brownian motion sampled on a unit grid.

### §1.5 BTR Prime consumption

`prime/` does not generate Renko bars; it consumes them via the
`nxr_sdk::Bar` type pushed over the `prime-nxr` WebSocket and the binary
mmap'd `.bar` files used in backtests. Each `Bar` carries
`(open_mts, close_mts, O, H, L, C, tick_count, kind = BarKind::Renko)`.

In `prime/crates/ml/src/renko_tracker.rs` a *separate* fixed-percentage
tracker is run at three scales $\lbrace 0.2\%, 0.5\%, 1.5\%\rbrace$ (crypto default) on
top of the already-Renko-sampled close series. This second pass produces
12 trend-state features per bar (signed direction EMA, log-streak EMA,
brick rate over a 60-bar window, and compression = current/avg brick size)
- scale-aware momentum features, not bars themselves. The adaptive
NXR-side Renko handles the information-time mapping; the fixed-pct tracker
provides multi-scale trend descriptors for the GBM feature matrix.
